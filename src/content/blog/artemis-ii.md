I wasn't expecting to care this much about a space mission.

I grew up on the tail end of the shuttle era. Space was something that happened in the background -- astronauts going to the ISS, satellites launching, none of it particularly dramatic. The last time humans went beyond low Earth orbit, my parents weren't even born yet. The Moon landings were history, not something that felt like it could happen again in my lifetime.

And then years of delays made it easy to tune out. Artemis kept slipping. SLS was over budget, behind schedule, the kind of program that invites cynicism. I followed it loosely, the way you follow a team you used to support -- checking in occasionally, never expecting much.

Artemis I changed that a little. Seeing an uncrewed Orion loop around the Moon and come back was genuinely impressive, but it still felt abstract. A test flight. A proof of concept. Hard to get emotional about an empty capsule.

Artemis II is different. There are people on board. Four astronauts are going to fly around the Moon and come back. That sentence alone is wild to me. No human has seen the far side of the Moon with their own eyes since 1972. No human has been that far from Earth in over fifty years. And now it's happening again.

I've been watching the coverage more closely than I expected. Reading about the crew, the mission profile, the risks. It's not just engineering -- it's the feeling of something resuming. Like a long pause in a conversation finally ending.

What gets me is the simplicity of it. They're not landing. They're not building a base. They're flying around the Moon and coming home. And somehow that's enough to make me feel like a kid again, the kind of kid who would have stayed up past bedtime to watch a launch.

I don't know what comes after this. Artemis III, a landing, maybe a sustained presence on the Moon. Maybe Mars eventually. I try not to get too far ahead. But right now, in this moment, humans are going back to the Moon. That's enough.

To the Moon.
