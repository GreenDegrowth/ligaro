---
title: "The Missing Green, The Missing Left"
description: "South Africa has the crises, the history, and the constituencies. What it doesn't have is the party."
pubDate: 2026-04-18
tags:

---

There's something about South African politics I've been trying to name, and I think it comes down to this: the obvious coalition doesn't exist. The country has a water system quietly coming apart -- the Vaal, Gqeberha running dry -- an economy built on digging things up, and some of the deepest inequality on earth. And yet there's no serious green party, and no serious left. A politics that connects the ecological crisis to the economy producing it should almost write itself here. Instead it's absent.

The green side is easier to explain. Environmentalism has been framed as a luxury question, and with unemployment sitting around a third of working-age adults, "stop the mine" sounds like "stop the jobs." The coal belt in Mpumalanga isn't just a carbon problem; it's a payroll, a union bloc, a big slice of foreign exchange. The argument is lost before it starts, because someone has already framed it as breathing versus eating. What gets left out is that the people most exposed to coal ash and dry taps are the same people the jobs argument claims to protect.

The left side is harder, because the machinery used to exist. The Tripartite Alliance had real roots. Most of that has been hollowed out since 1994. The SACP has been absorbed into the state and gone quiet. COSATU fractured after Marikana, where NUM had already lost the platinum belt and the police shot thirty-four miners. What filled the vacuum wasn't a recovered labour politics. It was the EFF, which borrows left vocabulary without the underlying commitments, and MK, which is personalist. The DA is liberal. There's no mass organisation that could carry a serious democratic-socialist programme here, let alone a red-green one. Plenty of people hold left-wing positions -- in unions, civics, universities -- but they have no electoral home.

Under all of it is a single question: who owns the economy that produces these crises? Eskom's problems are a governance story, but they're also an ownership story. The Just Energy Transition Partnership is billions handed down from richer countries under conditions that rhyme uncomfortably with structural adjustment. A transition owned by asset managers in New York and London isn't the same transition as one owned here. The technology might be identical. The intention shapes everything downstream.

I don't think this politics is impossible in South Africa. Union histories, commons traditions, a constitution that already names socio-economic rights -- the ground is better than in most places. It's a gap, not a ceiling. Gaps get filled.
